# Jimmy Interview — Project Manager (TLG Homes)

**Interviewee:** Jimmy, Project Manager at TLG Homes
**Interviewer:** Jason Christiansen

---

## Role & Responsibilities

- **Role:** Project Manager at TLG Homes
- **Scope:** Takes over once basement is in and backfilled — no involvement in land acquisition, excavation, or foundation
- **Handoff Point:** When lumber shows up on site
- **Daily Work:** Reviews blueprints with framer for any changes/modifications, then runs the project from lumber arrival through to completion
- **Active Projects:** 10–12 active builds currently, ~15–16 total including planning/design stages

## Typical Work Week

- **Mornings:** Office work — review schedule, handle calls/texts/emails, do scheduling for the week
- **Afternoons:** Site visits to verify work is being done correctly, "put out fires"
- **Successful week:** Everything scheduled gets done correctly with no rework
- **Terrible week:** Uncontrollable events — weather (snow, blizzards, freezing, high winds, heavy rain), which can cause damage (flooding, blown-off shingles, ruined flooring)
- **First thing every morning:** Opens Google Drive schedule spreadsheet — each property labeled by address with ~30 line items (every step of building a house). Keeps 2+ weeks ahead so subs have plenty of notice.

## Communication

- **Phone calls:** Easy day ~5, busy day ~20
- **Texts:** Good day ~10, bad day 50+
- **Emails:** Used for non-time-sensitive scheduling (3 weeks out)
- **Phone/text preferred** for urgent needs — subs are on the job and won't see email quickly
- **Most calls from:** Not trade-specific, but personality-specific — some subs call about everything; Paul the painter is the most demanding

## Priority & Decision Making

- **Site visit priority:** High-priority first (e.g., a house closing soon, regardless of distance). Otherwise, starts in Manitowoc and works north.
- **Has strong grasp of schedule** — studies it like a "Bible" morning, night, and before every site visit
- **Fires:** Unexpected urgent items that derail the day's plan — happen a couple times a week
- **When 5 things go wrong:** Discusses with team (Brian & Clint), divides and conquers

## Trade-by-Trade Stories

### Concrete (worst)
- Bitter cold winter (-30°F for 2 weeks). Kept digging/pouring foundations thinking they had it figured out with heating. Come spring thaw, 3 garage floors sank (became concave) and cracked.
- **Cost:** Company ate it. **Lesson:** Will hold off in questionable weather going forward.
- **Other issue:** Concrete subs sometimes skip compaction steps → settlement. Hard to catch without being there.

### Framing
- No horror stories — just minor things.

### Plumbing
- Sheetrockers occasionally put screws through water lines (minor, fixable)
- Drain lines in garage floors installed with wrong pitch (concrete crew pushes down on lines during gravel compaction)
- **Fix:** Now set drain lines on a bed of gravel so they don't settle

### Electrical
- Best trade — the electrician is also a county inspector, so he knows code inside out
- Issues only on custom homes — switch/light misplacement due to homeowner walkthrough communication gaps
- If electrician's fault, no cost to TLG; if homeowner changes mind, TLG pays

### HVAC
- Minor issue: Contractor was painting metal vent hoods himself without proper prep (no sanding, wrong primer/paint) → paint chipping/flaking
- Took 5 callbacks before discovering the root cause
- **Fix:** Switched to plastic vent hoods that come pre-colored

### Roofing
- One leak from improper flashing where shingles meet flat wall — caught early (house still in rough-in stage), no real damage

### Drywall
- Plaster system is water-based — shrinks and cracks as it cures (especially corners where walls meet ceiling)
- Standard callback within first year; tell homeowners to wait a year then do one big touch-up
- **Prevention:** Sheetrockers now hold screws back 8–12" from corners so ends can move → fewer cracks

### Painters
- **Old painter:** Taped and sprayed trim (great finish) but overspray everywhere — Jimmy and Clint had to scrub it off floors, tubs, cabinets
- **New painter:** Brushes trim (no overspray) but won't find his own touch-ups — expects Jimmy to mark blemishes for him
- Both painters resist painting garages and basement stairwells
- **Root cause:** Good labor is hard to find; messages from the boss don't always reach the worker on site

### Flooring
- Uses Macos — professional installers, lifetime warranty, no issues

### Cabinets / Trim / Joinery
- Issue: Trim package shows up unprimed when it was supposed to be primed (different supplier, miscommunication)
- Extra step: Send to painter for priming → install → painter returns for final coat
- **Fix:** Now knows to ask the question upfront

### Landscaping
- TLG doesn't do landscaping — rough grade only (excavator does final grading per drainage plan). Grass/flower beds on homeowner.

## Inspections

- **Varies drastically by municipality:** Two Rivers and Howard have the toughest inspectors; some municipalities are so lax they don't even know if inspection happened
- Newer inspectors (early 30s, fresh out of school) tend to write long lists citing specific building codes
- Seasoned inspectors focus on major items
- **Pattern recognition:** If a municipality flags the same issue 2-3 times, make it standard practice everywhere
- **No pre-inspection walkthrough:** Jimmy/Clint/Brian are on site near-daily, so they catch issues during the build, not at final

## Pain Points

- **Hardest info to get:** Engineering calculations (when framing issues require engineer sign-off) — engineers work on their own clock, can delay projects 2-7 days → domino effect
- **Biggest delays currently:** Plumbers (new company, owned by TLG owner, so harder to hold accountable)
- **Missing info:** Interior selections (paint colors, flooring, trim stain, hardware, stone) — no centralized database kept current by the designer → Jimmy gets caught without answers when subs ask
- **Created a selections spreadsheet** in Google Drive as a workaround, but it's not maintained
- **Invoice creep:** Subs who get comfortable start creeping prices up — needs constant vigilance
- **Buyer personality:** The 50% of buyers who are hyper-anal ("flashlight and magnifying glass") cause disproportionate work — would screen them out if possible

## If He Had an Assistant

- **Follow up with subs** — confirm they arrived, completed the day's work (so he doesn't have to drive there or call)
- **Scheduling** — data entry feels like a waste of his time, AI/tech could handle it
- **Remind him** that everything works out in the end

## Reflection

- **Hardest part of job:** Staying positive — not letting people know they get to you
- **Biggest waste of time:** Driving around to see if people showed up
- **Most stress:** Weather — unforeseen weather events
- **Never want to do again:** Anything involving water (silt socks, pumping foundations, ripping up water-damaged flooring/drywall/insulation)
- **Biggest accepted problem:** Subpar quality/craftsmanship — hard to find reliable people who do A+ work. Easy to say "good enough" and move on.
- **Attendance/tardiness:** Construction labor is often not held to the same standard as white-collar jobs — college education push has reduced the labor pool for trades
- **Owner cares about:** Profit margin ("if you're not making money, none of us have a job")
